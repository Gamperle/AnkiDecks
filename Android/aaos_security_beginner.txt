# 5.1 AAOS Security Fundamentals
# ==========================================

# --- Automotive Threat Model ---

What is an automotive threat model?	A structured analysis of attack vectors targeting vehicle systems — including untrusted third-party apps, vehicle bus attacks (CAN injection), network exposure (cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth), physical access to ports (OBD-II, USB), and supply chain compromise. It identifies assets (vehicle control, user data, safety functions), threat actors (hobbyists, criminals, nation-states), and attack surfaces unique to connected vehicles.
Why is the automotive threat model different from a typical mobile threat model?	Vehicles have safety-critical systems (brakes, steering) reachable from the infotainment network, long lifecycles (10–15 years), limited update opportunities, physical accessibility (public parking), and regulatory requirements (UNECE WP.29). A compromised IVI could potentially affect vehicle safety, unlike a compromised phone.
What are the main attack surfaces of an AAOS-based IVI system?	(1) Wireless interfaces: cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, V2X. (2) Wired interfaces: USB, OBD-II, Ethernet. (3) Application layer: untrusted apps from Play Store or sideloading. (4) Vehicle bus: CAN/Ethernet bridge between IVI and safety ECUs. (5) OTA update channel. (6) Cloud/backend services. (7) Companion device (phone) connections.
What is the principle of defense in depth applied to AAOS?	Layering multiple independent security controls so that failure of one does not compromise the system. In AAOS: secure boot (hardware trust), SELinux (mandatory access control), app sandboxing (process isolation), VHAL permission checks (API authorization), gateway ECU firewalling (network segmentation), and OTA signing (update integrity).
Who are the typical threat actors targeting vehicles?	(1) Hobbyist hackers: curiosity-driven, local physical access. (2) Thieves: relay attacks on digital keys, CAN injection for theft. (3) Organized criminals: fleet ransomware, data theft. (4) Nation-state actors: supply chain attacks, surveillance. (5) Malicious insiders: compromised OEM/supplier employees. (6) Researchers: responsible disclosure of vulnerabilities.
What vehicle assets need protection in the automotive threat model?	(1) Safety-critical functions: steering, braking, acceleration. (2) Vehicle access: door locks, ignition, digital keys. (3) User privacy data: location history, driving behavior, contacts. (4) Intellectual property: OEM firmware, calibration data. (5) Financial: EV charging billing, in-app purchases. (6) Availability: preventing denial-of-service to critical systems.

# --- Secure Boot and Verified Boot ---

What is secure boot for an IVI (In-Vehicle Infotainment) system?	A hardware-rooted process that verifies each stage of the boot chain — the SoC ROM verifies the bootloader, the bootloader verifies the kernel, and the kernel verifies the system/vendor partitions using cryptographic signatures. If any stage fails verification, the device refuses to boot.
What is verified boot (AVB) and how does it apply to AAOS?	Android Verified Boot (AVB) uses dm-verity to cryptographically verify every block of system/vendor/product partitions at read time. If a block has been tampered with, the read fails. This ensures the running OS has not been modified after flashing.
What is dm-verity?	A Linux kernel feature that provides transparent block-level integrity checking using a hash tree (Merkle tree). Each block's hash is verified against a pre-computed tree rooted in a signed root hash. Used by AVB to protect read-only partitions.
What is the root of trust in the IVI boot chain?	A hardware-immutable component (ROM code burned into the SoC) that holds the public key used to verify the first-stage bootloader. Since it cannot be modified, it forms the unbreakable foundation of the entire trust chain.
What happens if boot verification fails on an AAOS device?	The device either refuses to boot entirely (strict policy), boots into a recovery mode for re-flashing, or displays a persistent warning to the user. The exact behavior depends on the OEM's implementation and whether the bootloader is locked or unlocked.
What is the difference between a locked and unlocked bootloader on AAOS?	A locked bootloader enforces verified boot — only OEM-signed images can run. An unlocked bootloader allows flashing custom images but typically displays a tamper warning, may disable certain security features, and should be impossible on production vehicles (unlike consumer phones).
Why should production AAOS vehicles never allow bootloader unlocking?	An unlocked bootloader allows arbitrary code execution on the IVI, which could be used to attack safety-critical systems connected via the vehicle bus. Unlike phones where the risk is personal, vehicle compromise can endanger occupants and other road users.

# --- App Permission Model ---

What types of app permissions exist on AAOS?	(1) Normal permissions: granted automatically at install (e.g., internet access). (2) Dangerous permissions: require user approval (e.g., location, microphone). (3) Signature permissions: only granted to apps signed with the platform key. (4) Privileged permissions: granted to apps in the /system/priv-app or /product/priv-app partitions. (5) Car-specific permissions: e.g., android.car.permission.CAR_SPEED, CAR_ENERGY, CAR_CONTROL_AUDIO_SETTINGS.
What are automotive-specific permissions in AAOS?	Permissions under the android.car.permission namespace that control access to vehicle data and functions — CAR_SPEED (read vehicle speed), CAR_ENERGY (battery/fuel data), CAR_HVAC (climate control), CAR_EXTERIOR_LIGHTS (light control), CAR_POWERTRAIN (drivetrain data), CAR_VENDOR_EXTENSION (OEM custom properties).
Why are car permissions more restrictive than standard Android permissions?	Vehicle properties can directly affect physical safety (e.g., controlling lights, HVAC, or reading driving data). Granting broad access to third-party apps would create unacceptable safety and privacy risks. Most car permissions require signature or privileged access levels.
What is a privileged app in AAOS and why does it matter for security?	An app installed in /system/priv-app or /product/priv-app that can request privileged-level permissions not available to regular Play Store apps. OEM apps (CarSettings, CarLauncher) are typically privileged. Security matters because a compromised privileged app has elevated access to vehicle data and system services.
Can a third-party Play Store app read vehicle speed on AAOS?	Not by default. The CAR_SPEED permission is typically restricted to system or privileged apps. Apps using the Car App Library for navigation receive limited vehicle data through controlled APIs (e.g., NavigationManagerCallback) without direct VHAL access.

# --- VHAL Access and Permission Zones ---

What controls access to VHAL properties from apps?	CarPropertyManager enforces permission checks — each VehicleProperty is mapped to a required Android permission. The permission level (normal, dangerous, signature, privileged) determines which apps can read or write each property. SELinux policies further restrict which processes can communicate with the VHAL daemon.
What is the difference between read-only and control properties in VHAL?	Read-only properties (e.g., vehicle speed, odometer) let apps observe vehicle state. Control properties (e.g., HVAC temperature set, door lock command) let apps actuate vehicle functions. Control properties require higher-privilege permissions and are typically restricted to system/OEM apps.
Why is restricting VHAL write access critical for safety?	A writable VHAL property can command a physical action in the vehicle (e.g., setting HVAC, controlling lights). If an untrusted app could write arbitrary properties, it could potentially interfere with vehicle systems or create driver distractions. Write permissions are therefore limited to trusted apps.
How does CarPropertyManager enforce per-property permissions?	When an app calls getProperty() or setProperty(), CarService checks the calling app's granted permissions against the permission requirement defined in the property's annotation. If the app lacks the required permission, a SecurityException is thrown.
What is the vendor property ID range and who can access vendor properties?	Vendor properties use IDs in the range 0x0100_0000–0x0FFF_FFFF. They are OEM-defined and invisible to standard AOSP code. Access is restricted to OEM apps with vendor-specific permissions. This prevents third-party apps from reading or controlling proprietary vehicle functions.

# --- SELinux Basics on AAOS ---

What is SELinux and why is it used on AAOS?	Security-Enhanced Linux — a mandatory access control (MAC) system that enforces security policies regardless of user permissions. On AAOS, it restricts which processes can access which files, sockets, devices, and services, preventing compromised processes from exceeding their defined capabilities.
What is the difference between MAC (Mandatory Access Control) and DAC (Discretionary Access Control)?	DAC (traditional Linux file permissions) lets file owners control access, meaning a compromised process running as a user can access anything that user can. MAC (SELinux) enforces system-wide policies independent of ownership — even root processes are confined to their allowed operations.
What is an SELinux domain in AAOS?	A label assigned to a running process that defines what resources it can access. AAOS defines domains like vehicle_hal (VHAL process), carservice (CarService), system_app, untrusted_app, etc. Each domain has explicitly enumerated permissions.
What is an SELinux type in AAOS?	A label assigned to files, sockets, ports, and other resources. SELinux policy rules define which domains can perform which operations on which types. For example, only the vehicle_hal domain can open the VHAL socket device.
What are SELinux neverallow rules?	Rules that explicitly prohibit certain access patterns and cannot be overridden by any allow rule. In AAOS, neverallow rules ensure untrusted apps can never directly access vehicle HAL devices, even if an OEM accidentally adds a permissive allow rule.
What is the difference between enforcing and permissive SELinux modes?	Enforcing mode blocks and logs all policy violations — this is the required production mode for AAOS. Permissive mode only logs violations without blocking them — used during development to discover needed policy rules.

# --- App Sandboxing and Process Isolation ---

What is app sandboxing on AAOS?	Each app runs in its own Linux process with a unique UID, separate file directories, and SELinux domain. Apps cannot directly access other apps' data, system processes, or hardware devices unless explicitly permitted through Android's permission framework.
How does Linux UID isolation work for AAOS apps?	Each installed app receives a unique Linux UID (e.g., u0_a123). File permissions and process isolation prevent one app from reading another app's private data directories (/data/data/<package>). This is the same mechanism as consumer Android.
What additional isolation does AAOS apply beyond standard Android sandboxing?	AAOS adds: (1) VHAL access restricted by car-specific permissions and SELinux policies. (2) CAN/vehicle bus completely inaccessible to app processes. (3) Certain system APIs locked to signature or privileged apps only. (4) Multi-user isolation separating driver profiles.
What is seccomp-bpf and how does it protect AAOS?	Secure Computing with Berkeley Packet Filter — a Linux kernel feature that restricts which system calls a process can invoke. Android applies seccomp filters to apps, blocking dangerous syscalls (e.g., reboot, mount, raw socket creation). This limits what a compromised app can do at the kernel level.

# --- Secure Update Basics ---

What is OTA signing and why is it important for AAOS?	OTA update payloads are cryptographically signed by the OEM's private key. The device verifies the signature using the embedded public key before applying the update. This prevents attackers from installing malicious firmware even if they compromise the OTA delivery channel.
What is rollback protection in AAOS OTA updates?	A mechanism that prevents downgrading to older, vulnerable firmware versions. The bootloader stores a rollback index that only increases. If an OTA payload has a lower rollback index than the current one, the device rejects it.
How do A/B updates enhance security?	A/B updates write the new image to the inactive partition slot while the current system runs. If the new image fails to boot (verification failure, corruption), the bootloader automatically falls back to the previous known-good slot — maintaining system availability.
What happens if an OTA update is interrupted mid-apply on AAOS?	With A/B updates, the currently running (active) slot is untouched during the update. An interruption simply leaves the inactive slot in an incomplete state. The device continues running the current version, and the update can be retried.
What is the role of vbmeta in OTA security?	vbmeta (Verified Boot Metadata) contains the hash tree root hashes and signing information for all verified partitions. During an OTA update, the new vbmeta is signed by the OEM key, ensuring the new partition hashes are authentic and unmodified.

# --- Vehicle Data Protection ---

What data does AAOS need to protect?	(1) Personal data: contacts, messages, call logs synced from phone. (2) Location and driving data: GPS history, routes, driving behavior. (3) Vehicle state: fuel/battery levels, mileage, diagnostic data. (4) User credentials: account tokens, saved passwords, Wi-Fi keys. (5) Media: downloaded music, playlists, preferences. (6) Payment info: EV charging billing, in-app purchases.
What is scoped storage on AAOS?	Scoped storage restricts apps to their own sandboxed file directories and provides mediated access to shared media (photos, audio, video) via MediaStore. Apps cannot browse arbitrary filesystem paths, preventing data exfiltration from other apps.
How does multi-user isolation protect vehicle data?	Each driver profile runs as a separate Android user with independent encrypted storage, app data, and preferences. One driver's personal data (contacts, media, navigation history) is cryptographically inaccessible to other driver profiles.
What is File-Based Encryption (FBE) on AAOS?	FBE encrypts each user's data with a unique key derived from their credentials. Data is split into Credential Encrypted (CE — accessible only after user unlock) and Device Encrypted (DE — accessible after boot). This ensures a stolen head unit cannot easily extract personal data.
What is the difference between CE and DE storage on AAOS?	CE (Credential Encrypted) storage is unlocked only after the user provides their PIN/pattern/password — it holds personal data like contacts and app data. DE (Device Encrypted) storage is accessible immediately after boot — it holds system data needed before user unlock, like alarm settings and device administration.

# --- User Authentication on AAOS ---

What authentication methods does AAOS support for driver profiles?	PIN (numeric code), pattern (swipe pattern on grid), and password (alphanumeric). These unlock the user's CE storage and grant access to their profile's apps and data.
Why is user authentication important for AAOS security?	Authentication protects personal data (contacts, navigation history, saved credentials) from unauthorized access when the vehicle is shared, valet-parked, or accessed by service technicians. It also gates access to administrative functions.
What security considerations exist for guest mode on AAOS?	Guest profiles should have no access to other users' data, limited vehicle control capabilities, restricted app installation, automatic data wipe on profile removal, and no persistence of browsing/navigation history to protect the primary user's privacy.
How does profile switching security work on AAOS?	When a user switches profiles, the previous user's CE storage is locked (re-encrypted), their processes are stopped, and the new user must authenticate to unlock their own CE storage. This ensures data isolation during transitions.
What challenges does user authentication create in an automotive context?	Authentication must balance security with usability — drivers should not face complex authentication while trying to drive. Solutions include: delayed lock (don't require auth for the first N minutes after engine start), trusted device bypass (paired phone as trusted device), biometric options (face unlock via cabin camera), and persistent auth during a driving session.

# --- USB and Debug Port Security ---

Why is ADB (Android Debug Bridge) dangerous on production AAOS vehicles?	ADB provides shell access, app installation/uninstallation, file transfer, and logcat access. An attacker with ADB access could install malware, extract personal data, modify system configurations, or use the IVI as a pivot point to attack the vehicle bus.
How should ADB be configured on production AAOS vehicles?	ADB should be completely disabled in user (production) builds. Only userdebug and eng builds (used during development) should have ADB enabled, and those builds should never ship in production vehicles. The USB debugging setting should be hidden or removed from production CarSettings.
What is the USB accessory/host security risk on AAOS?	USB ports can be used to connect malicious devices (BadUSB/rubber ducky attacks that emulate keyboards, rogue network adapters). AAOS should restrict USB device classes to approved types (media storage, audio) and disable USB debugging interfaces in production.
How can OEMs lock down development interfaces on production vehicles?	(1) Disable ADB in production builds. (2) Remove or fuse JTAG/serial debug ports on production hardware. (3) Restrict USB device class access. (4) Disable adb over Wi-Fi/network. (5) Enforce SELinux policies that block debug tools. (6) Use secure boot to prevent loading debug-enabled firmware.
What is a USB security policy for AAOS?	A configuration that defines which USB device types are allowed: mass storage (for media), Android Auto (for phone projection), specific audio accessories — while blocking HID devices (keyboard/mouse emulation), network adapters, and ADB connections in production mode.

# --- Privacy in Connected Vehicles ---

What personal data do connected vehicles collect?	Location and GPS tracks, driving behavior (speed, braking, acceleration patterns), cabin microphone recordings (voice commands), camera images (cabin monitoring, dashcam), contacts and messages (synced from phone), entertainment preferences, and charging habits (EVs).
What is GDPR and how does it apply to AAOS vehicles in the EU?	The General Data Protection Regulation requires that vehicle data collection has a legal basis (consent, legitimate interest), users can access/delete their data, data minimization is practiced, and data transfers outside the EU comply with adequacy requirements. Applies to any OEM selling connected vehicles in the EU.
What is CCPA and how does it apply to AAOS vehicles in California?	The California Consumer Privacy Act gives consumers the right to know what personal data is collected, request its deletion, opt out of data sales, and not face discrimination for exercising privacy rights. Applies to OEMs above revenue/data-volume thresholds selling vehicles in California.
What is user consent flow for data collection on AAOS?	A transparent process where the driver is informed about what data is collected (location, driving behavior, voice recordings), why it's collected (navigation, safety, analytics), who it's shared with (OEM, insurers, advertisers), and can opt in or out at any time through CarSettings.
What is data minimization in the automotive context?	Collecting only the minimum data necessary for a stated purpose. For example, an AAOS navigation app needs current location but not historical driving patterns. Telemetry services should anonymize or aggregate data before transmission when individual identification is unnecessary.
What privacy measures should AAOS implement when a vehicle is resold?	Factory reset that cryptographically erases all user data (CE storage keys destroyed), clears navigation history and paired devices, resets all accounts, and removes personal media. The vehicle should behave as a new device for the next owner.

# --- Bluetooth and Wi-Fi Attack Surface ---

What Bluetooth security risks exist for AAOS?	(1) BlueBorne-style attacks: remote code execution via Bluetooth stack vulnerabilities. (2) Unauthorized pairing: impersonation attacks during device pairing. (3) PBAP/MAP data access: unauthorized contact and message download after pairing. (4) Bluetooth tracking: persistent device addresses enabling vehicle tracking. (5) Eavesdropping: intercepting hands-free calls.
How does Bluetooth pairing security work on AAOS?	AAOS uses Secure Simple Pairing (SSP) with numeric comparison — both devices display a 6-digit code that the user verifies. This prevents man-in-the-middle attacks during pairing. LE Secure Connections uses ECDH key exchange for stronger protection.
What Wi-Fi attack vectors apply to AAOS?	(1) Rogue hotspot/evil twin: tricking the IVI into connecting to a malicious access point mimicking a known network. (2) Deauth attacks: forcing disconnection. (3) Credential theft: capturing Wi-Fi passwords from saved networks. (4) Network-based attacks once connected: man-in-the-middle, traffic interception.
How can AAOS mitigate rogue hotspot attacks?	Use WPA3 for all connections, avoid auto-connecting to previously seen SSIDs without verification, implement 802.11w (Management Frame Protection) to resist deauth attacks, and route sensitive traffic through VPN or TLS tunnels regardless of network trust.
What is Bluetooth address randomization and why is it important for AAOS?	LE Privacy feature where the Bluetooth adapter periodically changes its broadcast address, preventing passive tracking of the vehicle's location via Bluetooth scanning. Without randomization, a vehicle's Bluetooth address is a persistent identifier that can be tracked.
What security measures should be taken when a phone is paired to an AAOS vehicle?	(1) Require explicit user confirmation for pairing. (2) Limit data access scope (choose which profiles: HFP, PBAP, MAP, A2DP). (3) Allow users to review and revoke paired devices. (4) Clear pairing data on factory reset. (5) Implement timeouts for unused pairings. (6) Warn when PBAP downloads contacts to vehicle storage.

# --- Third-Party App Vetting ---

What is the Play Store automotive review process?	Apps submitted to the Play Store's automotive track undergo additional review for distraction compliance (template usage, UX restriction adherence), restricted API usage, automotive-specific quality guidelines, and safety impact assessment. Apps must use the Car App Library and comply with driver distraction rules.
What is the restricted API surface for AAOS Play Store apps?	Third-party apps cannot directly access VHAL properties, CAN bus, or most car-specific permissions. They are limited to Car App Library templates, standard Android APIs, and the subset of CarManager APIs available to non-privileged apps (e.g., basic UX restriction queries).
Why is sideloading prevention important on AAOS?	Sideloaded apps bypass Play Store review, can request dangerous permissions, may not comply with driver distraction guidelines, and could contain malware targeting the vehicle. Production AAOS builds should disable "install from unknown sources" and the setting should not be exposed to users.
How do OEMs vet pre-installed system apps on AAOS?	OEMs should conduct security reviews of all bundled apps (including supplier-provided apps), verify they request only necessary permissions, test for vulnerabilities and data leaks, validate SELinux policy compliance, and maintain a software bill of materials (SBOM) for supply chain tracking.
What risks do pre-installed bloatware apps pose on AAOS?	Bloatware apps run with elevated privileges (system/priv-app), have broader permissions than Play Store apps, and may contain vulnerabilities that persist across OTA updates. Compromised bloatware can access vehicle data, user information, and system services that third-party apps cannot reach.
